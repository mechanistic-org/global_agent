# Erik Cameron Norris — A Life in Constraint

He was born at El Camino Hospital in Mountain View, California, on April 20, 1968. Earth Monkey, if you're counting. His father was Carroll Boyd Norris III, then the youngest physics professor Stanford had ever appointed. The marriage lasted approximately a year. His mother packed what she had, found her way back to her college sweetheart — Jerry Gene Lincicum, a man who would become Erik's Dad in every way that mattered — and together they loaded a Morris Minor and drove north.

North meant Surrey, British Columbia. Five acres of an old farm: the main buildings, old hog pens, chicken coops, the particular smell of land that has been worked and left and is being worked again. His mother joined the Parent-Teacher Association. His Nana Freda Diane MacGregor walked with him at Blackie Spit along the tidal flats, teaching him without teaching him to read what the water leaves behind. He caught tadpoles in Crescent Park with his best friend Lorna Schouten. He went to Crescent Park Elementary. He was, by most measures, a child.

Then his parents made a decision that would shape everything: they moved to Lund, a remote coastal settlement at the end of Highway 101 — the literal end of the road — to help start the Spirit Cove Oyster Cooperative with a group of friends and family. Erik was seven years old.

What Lund gave him was not school. Formal education came by correspondence, and he burned through it as fast as he could because it was sometimes interesting, most often boring, but had to be done. The real curriculum was physical and immediate: outboard motors and tugboat engines and bilge pumps and cable suspension winches and spat harvest. The tidal rhythms of shellfish farming. The particular patience required to work with living systems that don't care about your schedule. The ocean as teacher — unforgiving, consistent, honest about consequences in a way that classrooms rarely are.

He also became an athlete. Division II semi-professional soccer in British Columbia. Member of the Provincial Track and Field team. A body trained alongside a mind — both running multiple processes, both learning that output is a function of applied force over sustained time.

At twelve he bought his first motorcycle.

His mother said: over my dead body.

He said: what if I pay for it?

She said: when you can pay for it, you can buy a bike.

Three months later he had felled, bucked, split, humped to the end of the driveway, and loaded into customers' trucks nineteen cords of wood at a hundred dollars a cord. Nineteen hundred dollars. Enough for the ferry rides to Vancouver and back to Lund. Enough for the bike at five hundred and fifty dollars. Enough for new tires, tune-up parts, a filter, and whatever remained was his.

This was not a story about a motorcycle. This was the operating system establishing its core parameters. Constraint identified. Resources inventoried. Solution designed. Work executed. The way you do anything is the way you do everything. He was twelve years old and he already knew this, even if he didn't have language for it yet.

He visited his biological father once, at eleven. Carroll Boyd Norris III by then worked at Sandia National Laboratories — the facility where the nuclear arsenal lives, where the physics of destruction is applied with the precision of engineering. The visit produced two objects. The first was a rose, frozen in liquid nitrogen and then shattered — matter behaving at a boundary condition, beautiful and then suddenly not, the physics made visceral. The second was a copy of a patent. A handheld particle beam weapon. His father gave it to him as a keepsake.

Erik still has it somewhere.

A man he met once, at eleven, handed him a frozen rose and a weapon patent and returned to his work. The afternoon contained a complete cosmology: precision, beauty, consequence, and the casual assumption that a child could hold these things and not be broken by them. Erik wasn't broken. He took notes.

---

When the Spirit Cove Oyster Cooperative failed — swallowed over the course of roughly two years by Japanese capital buying out every independent operator along the coast, one by one, until there was nothing left — Erik was fourteen. He didn't want to be a logger. He didn't want to be a fisherman. The cooperative was gone and the life it had organized was gone with it.

He moved south to Palo Alto to live with Grandma Vera — Jerry Lincicum's mother, 783 Sutter Street, midtown. Old Arizona money. Depression money. Dust Bowl money, which is a different kind of money than money that has never been tested — it has a particular density and a particular fragility simultaneously, the way materials behave when they've been stressed past their yield point and survived. Vera was amazing in the way that people who have carried real historical weight are sometimes amazing. She was also, by then, getting crazy in ways that would only deepen. Two years at 783 Sutter. He doesn't say much about it. Some constraint environments you survive by not cataloguing every failure mode.

Uncle David — Jerry's brother — ran Menlo Park Exxon. Erik worked there. He learned the gas station the way he had learned everything else: by touching the thing, by understanding the system, by being present when it broke and staying until it worked again.

At sixteen he moved into a studio apartment on Alma Street. He lied about his age to get it. His girlfriend at the time — Julie, a Flamenco dancer, a sophomore at Stanford — was the primary on the lease. He had met her at the gas station. She drove a pink 1959 Rambler Classic. He pumped her gas.

The gas station kid from the oyster farm at the end of the highway, sleeping in apartments held by borrowed identities, falling for Flamenco dancers in vintage cars. He was building a life with whatever tolerance the world allowed.

He kept working. He kept wrenching. He enrolled at De Anza College — twenty-two units a quarter, the Associate's Degree in Automotive Engine Performance, the Outstanding Academic Achievement Certificate, the Elks National Foundation Vocational Grant. When the schedule required it, he slept in the De Anza library. The library was warm and had books.

The books were not the correspondence curriculum. He had been reading the real ones since single digits: Heinlein and Dick and Asimov and Bradbury, worlds built from pure constraint logic, ideas as protagonist, physics as plot. Carroll's *Jabberwocky* — not nonsense but proof that grammar and meaning are separable, that structure can outlast sense, that language has its own physics. Brautigan's strange pastoral dream of cybernetic meadows written in 1967 before anyone knew what they were dreaming. These were not the influences of a man who later became an engineer. They were the same obsession in a different key.

His mother tells people his first words were "iambic pentameter." Not a thing. A *pattern*. A structural description of how meaning moves through language. She delivers this information with exasperated pride — the particular expression of a mother who has just understood what she's made.

---

His first job in technology was 1985, at EP Technologies in Sunnyvale. The company was building the EPT-1000 Cardiac Ablation System — a treatment for irregular heartbeat delivered through minimally invasive catheters. He started as an electronics technician doing assembly and rework. Component-level troubleshooting. Hands on the thing that was broken until it worked. In 1987 he was promoted to Electronics Production Supervisor.

He was nineteen. The work was small enough to hold in your hands and precise enough to kill a patient if you got it wrong. Constraint environments that don't forgive mistakes. This would become a theme.

Simultaneously he managed Alice's Garage in Woodside — computer-engine feedback systems, late-model driveability diagnostics. The car as a legible system. Input, process, output, failure mode, root cause. The same epistemology applied to combustion that he applied to cardiac catheters. He was not building a career. He was developing a method.

In 1989 Silicon Graphics was riding high and he was looking for new challenges. He joined SGI's Entry Systems Division as a Technician IV — general support for three hardware groups. Within a year he was a Junior Mechanical Designer, learning Pro/ENGINEER and Flotherm, building thermal and mechanical solutions for workstations that consumed power the way race engines consume fuel. Five products from concept to production: the Personal Iris, the Indigo, the Indigo II, the Indy. Machines that a generation of engineers and scientists and artists used to do their best work.

He had gotten the SGI job through Jen — the woman who would become his wife for thirteen years, who he met while she was a Stanford student getting her Master's in Product Design at what was then the nascent Stanford D-school. He was still wrenching for William Michael Automotive when she told him about the opening. He took it as a second job. The dual track — technology and mechanics — didn't begin as strategy. It began because a woman he loved mentioned a job and he said yes without stopping the other thing.

---

In 1993 he left SGI and founded Mechanistic. The name was deliberate. It means something about the way he sees the world: as systems, as mechanisms, as structures with knowable properties operating according to discoverable rules. He was twenty-five, living in La Honda, working alone, integrating seamlessly into client development teams while maintaining his own sovereign infrastructure.

Over the next decade he touched twenty-two successful commercial products from start to finish. SwitchBlade and Frantic Frames inline speed skates. The Sunbeam Toast Logic Window Toaster. The Acer Aspire telephone. The Microsoft Xbox. The HP Jornada 430. The Motorola MP3 player. The Netscreen-500 security appliance. The KaVo X-Unit Dental System. Products that people held in their hands and used and sometimes loved without knowing his name. This is the nature of the work. The maker disappears into the made thing.

He also owned and operated Red Automotive Repair in Mountain View — general repair and racing specialty, setups for SCCA SOLO and the NASA Camaro/Mustang Challenge. He was a competitive SCCA SoloII OSP class autocross racer. He was running a consultancy and a repair shop and racing cars and designing products simultaneously. The parallel processing was not new. The parallel processing was constitutional.

The early 2000s brought the end of the marriage. A divorce from Jen in early 2002. And then, in September of that year, something that sounds administrative but was not: he legally changed his name from Lincicum to Norris. Not a reinvention. A return. Erik Cameron Norris — the name on the birth certificate from El Camino Hospital in Mountain View — restored. The stepfather's name had stuck for thirty-four years. He changed it back to match what had always been true.

Some commits don't have a `git revert`. Some do. This one did.

---

In 2003 he joined Digidesign — the professional audio company, makers of Pro Tools, the software that records most of the music that has been recorded in the last thirty years. His job was the physical architecture of the tools that make the tools: the ICON, the D-Command, the C|24, the Mbox 2, the 003. Digital audio workstations used by producers and engineers to make records. He was building the physical infrastructure of music. He was also, as system administrator for the company's Pro/INTRALINK and Windchill CAD servers, already wrestling with the problem of persistent, auditable, durable professional data. He didn't have that language for it yet. The problem was already there.

He married Ann on May 6, 2006.

In 2008 he joined Kaleidescape — high-end home audiovisual servers and players, products built for people who care about the difference between experiencing something and merely consuming it. He was the sole mechanical engineer for several years. Six generations of products designed by one person who understood that the physical object is not separate from the experience it enables. The Mini System. The Cinema One. The Orpheus.

His daughter Anika was born in September 2008.

Around 2010 he discovered photography — landscape, macro, portrait, studio, weddings, a portable photobooth he engineered himself for parties. The same eye that tracked tolerance stacks and thermal budgets turned toward light and time and the way a face holds its history. He was riding approximately ten thousand miles a year on the bike before the pandemic. He snapped both Achilles tendons at different times — one while traveling for work in China. Had them repaired. Kept riding.

Kaleidescape laid him off in March 2015. He moved to Avegant and designed the Glyph — a wearable video headset that projected images directly onto the retina through beam-splitting optics. He was engineering the precise boundary between the physical world and human perception. He had been doing this in some form his whole life.

Noon home followed — a connected smart light switch, regulatory compliance, the second generation product designed and validated before the company was gone. Then Hyphen — automated foodservice makelines, robots making burritos with the tolerance precision of aerospace assembly, because a burrito is a constraint problem if you care about consistency at scale and the physical world does not forgive.

---

In August 2022 he returned to Mechanistic. But he was not the same person who had founded it in La Honda in 1993. Four decades of constraint environments had accumulated into something that was not just technical expertise. It was a theory of systems. It was a forensic epistemology applied across every domain he had touched — engines, catheters, workstations, optics, food robots, musical instruments, bicycles, photographs, bodies.

He had also begun to understand something about the systems that were now making decisions about people like him. The ATS platforms — the automated hiring systems that parsed resumes and ranked candidates before any human saw them — had been built by software people who had never held a thermal resistance budget or managed an injection molding tolerance stack. Their ontologies had no vocabulary for the work he had done for forty years. He was invisible by design. Not failing a screen. Never screened at all.

He called it Round Zero. The silent elimination before the game starts.

And he built MootMoat to fix it — an open standard for agentic professional identity. A sovereignty protocol. A way for engineers with real constraints and real scars to become legible to systems that would otherwise render them invisible. The Depth Signals. The Tri-Node Footprint. The conformance levels. It is Niven's physics thinking applied to the identity problem: what are the actual forces at work here? What does the system actually do? Build from first principles. No dark factory. Full paper trail. Every decision auditable after the fact.

Around the same time he solved a different problem: how do you give an AI agent durable, reliable memory that survives across sessions, machine restarts, complete environment teardowns? The answer he arrived at was git. Not as a workaround. As the natural substrate. Every property you need for persistent agentic context — durability, auditability, diffing, branching, multi-party write access — git already has. The agents boot cold, read a GitHub Issue, execute one task, post a comment, and die. The container burns. The commit survives.

He sent Linus Torvalds a LinkedIn connection request to tell him this. The note referenced what git means in British English — a stupid, irritating, or contemptible person, depending on context, which is exactly what Linus has said he named it after — because eighteen months living in England will teach you things that matter in unexpected moments. Linus has not yet accepted.

The request is still pending.

---

He is fifty-seven years old. He was born at El Camino Hospital in Mountain View to a physicist who froze roses and filed patents for particle beam weapons and then returned to his work. He was raised on an oyster farm at the end of a highway in British Columbia by a man who wasn't his father and loved him anyway. He left at fourteen with nowhere particular to go and built a life with whatever the tolerance allowed — gas station to library floor to studio apartment on Alma Street to five products at SGI to twenty-two products at Mechanistic to six generations of Kaleidescape hardware to the retina-projecting Glyph to the git-backed agent swarm running on infrastructure Linus Torvalds built in 1991 without knowing that's what he was building.

The first words were a structural description of how meaning moves through language. The operating system has not changed. The tooling is considerably better.

The scars are the receipt. The Source does not go back. The laptop does.

The commit survives.
